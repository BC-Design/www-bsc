Wheatstone Bridge fundamentals:

via: http://www.electronics-tutorials.ws/blog/wheatstone-bridge.html

"Balanced bridge circuits find many useful electronics applications such as being used to measure changes in light intensity, pressure or strain. The types of resistive sensors that can be used within a wheatstone bridge circuit include: photoresistive sensors (LDR’s), positional sensors (potentiometers), piezoresistive sensors (strain gauges) and temperature sensors (thermistor’s), etc.

There are many wheatstone bridge applications for sensing a whole range of mechanical and electrical quantities, but one very simple wheatstone bridge application is in the measurement of light by using a photoresistive device. One of the resistors within the bridge network is replaced by a light dependent resistor, or LDR.

The Wheatstone Bridge has many uses in electronic circuits other than comparing an unknown resistance with a known resistance. When used with Operational Amplifiers, the Wheatstone bridge circuit can be used to measure and amplify small changes in resistance, RX due, for example, to changes in light intensity as we have seen above."

via: https://answers.yahoo.com/question/index?qid=20090708185125AAgq12c

This puts it well:

"Using a bridge to measure an unknown resistance instead of using an Ohmmeter is like using a balance and a set of calibrated weights to measure an unknown weight instead of using a spring scale. "

Even with inaccurate instruments, reading zero is easy!

A voltmeter or ammeter hooked across a Wheatstone Bridge reads zero when the test resistance equals the reference resistance.

This can be useful when the resistance could go above or below the reference, e.g. with strain gauge displacement.

5.11 Bridge Circuits

"A basic Wheatstone bridge circuit generally operates with a disbalanced bridge. This is called the deflection method of measurement. It is based on detecting the voltage across the bridge diagonal. The bridge output voltage is a nonlinear function of a disbalance Δ, where the sensor's resistance Rv = R(1 + Δ).  However, for small change (Δ < 0.05), which often is the case, the bridge output may be considered to be quasi-linear.  The bridge maximum sensitivity is obtained when R1 = R2 and R3 = R.

http://www.ametherm.com/thermistor/ntc-thermistors-temperature-measurement-with-wheatstone-bridge

"The major challenge of accurate resistance measurement is to alleviate the loading effect of the circuit by the meter.  Such an inaccuracy is caused by the drawing of power by the meter, although the amount of power drawn is negligibly small, from the circuit, even if it has a very high-impedance (e.g. 10 MΩ).

For accurate measurement of Rx, the Wheatstone bridge plays a great role, as the galvanometer, in balanced condition, does not draw any power from the circuit. This increases accuracy by alleviating the "loading" effect of the circuit by the meter."
