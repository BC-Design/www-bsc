== Pitch BIS to THW, D-Lab

Hello THW!

Tomorrow at noon (lunch provided) is the last infosession for a student-run consulting program that could use some data scientists.

You might be familiar with the Berkeley Energy and Resources Collaborative (BERC) through Cleanweb Berkeley or the Cleanweb Hackathon.  BERC Innovative Solutions (BIS) is a student-led cleantech consulting program, in which teams of four take on semester-long pro bono consulting projects for cleantech companies.  Client applicants this year include Amazon Web Services, SunPower, Electric Power Research Institute, National Renewable Energy Laboratory, PG&E, and 20(!) more.  The estimated time commitment is 3-5 hours per week.  The program can be a great way to learn about the cleantech space and make connections with cleantech professionals.

A few projects in particular might be interesting to data-centric folks:

Silver Spring Networks

Help identify new Internet of Things cases which take advantage of our new network capabilities, which fall outside our traditional energy and streetlight businesses

Silver Spring Networks

Identify new markets for distributed monitoring and control of energy production systems, e.g. rooftop solar

National Renewable Energy Laboratory

Improve the accessibility of data by creating a "standardized data landscape" for distributed solar; focus on the adoption of industry-led open data standards and the ability to make machine-readable data accessible from sources that are not currently machine-readable; identify key datasets, identify/create software for data scraping, work with NREL to get data into a Solar Data Exchange platform.

National Renewable Energy Laboratory

Improve understanding of the regulatory challenges for air pollutant emissions permitting of biorefineries (refineries producing biofuels) and how those challenges affect biorefinery design, decision-making and cost. Develop database of relevant biorefinery permitting/pollution control/emissions data; analyze the database and obtain estimates of quantified benefits (e.g., reduced permitting process duration) of implementing improvements.

The Energy Coalition

Create a Playbook for Microgrid Scoping based on existing microgrids to allow policy markers to determine the relevant viability of different microgrid architectures.  Geospatial market analysis and visualization of Southern California neighborhoods that intersect multiple datasets from CalEnviroscreen, certain demographic data, public information and proprietary information in order to prioritize disadvantaged communities in the most need of assistance.  Other factors include the geographic distribution of assets owned by large property owners, as well as the types of buildings, occupants, lease types, and metering configurations.  Work with LBNL's Distributed Energy Resources Customer Adoption Model (DER-CAM).

I didn't say that "all content should be free".  I believe that we should have good photographs of culturally and scientifically important people, places, and things, free of licensing restrictions, to enable the creation of free educational materials and other resources.  Technology now enables an amateur to capture, losslessly copy, and distribute competent photographs at relatively little cost.  Maybe the professionals believe them to be inferior, but if the products that these amateurs freely give away is acceptable to the market as a replacement good for works with licensing restrictions, people will use the free version instead.

On the other hand, no one is _obligated_ to photograph my wedding, a property I'm trying to sell, or anything else for free.  If there is a market for something a professional captures, that's great, but it's up to the professional to find a compelling value proposition or find something else to do.  How many people make a living playing the saxophone, riding horses, writing poetry, blacksmithing, mountaineering?  How many 'gentleman scientists' are left, self-funded or supported by a wealthy patron?  It's not like the old days, but a few have found niches and made it work.  Technological advances and cultural trends change the commercial viability of careers, whether we like it or not.

Andy Colwell: Can you expand on how digital images are treated differently than other digital goods? I don't see how it's "anyone with a camera" any more than it's "anyone with a microphone" or "anyone with a keyboard".  Most of the writing, music, and visual art that's created by amateurs does not have a substantial audience, but some of it does, and the Internet makes it easy for successful work to spread.  Duplication, modification, and transmission are essentially free, so permissively-licensed content has a competitive advantage.  This is why the Linux operating system owns the server and embedded device markets, and it's the same conversation academics are now having about open access research publication.

The creator only gets to decide what something is worth within the bounds set by the market, and "free" is always an option.  Donald Trump gets to use a free photo of Donald Trump, but so does everyone else.  Unless you can get all creators everywhere to band together and agree to never work for free—and people work for free for all kinds of reasons—then what can you do?
